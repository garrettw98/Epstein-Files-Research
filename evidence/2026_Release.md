## The Redaction Scandal (Expanded)

### What Went Wrong

**The "Soft Redaction" Error**:
- Digital files contained black boxes over text
- Users could **copy-paste** to reveal hidden names
- Millions downloaded unredacted versions before fixes

### Wall Street Journal Investigation

The WSJ found:
- **43+ victims' full names exposed**
- Including **24+ who were minors** when abused
- Some names appeared **100+ times**
- **Home addresses** visible in keyword searches

### Attorney Findings

Attorneys for survivors found:
- **At least 31 people** victimized as children had names exposed
- Information promised to be protected was publicly released

### Victim Response

A group of survivors wrote:
> "This latest release of Jeffrey Epstein files is being sold as transparency, but what it actually does is expose survivors. Once again, survivors are having their names and identifying information exposed, while **the men who abused us remain hidden and protected**."

### Legal Action

**February 1, 2026**: Attorneys representing **200+ alleged victims** asked federal judges to:
- **Immediately shut down** the DOJ Epstein Files website
- Called it *"the single most egregious violation of victim privacy in one day in United States history"*

### DOJ Response

Deputy AG Todd Blanche defended the effort:
> "Think about this though: you're talking about pieces of paper that stack from the ground to **two Eiffel Towers**. The minute that a victim or their lawyer reached out to us since Friday, we immediately dealt with it and pulled it down."

**Reality**: As of February 4, 2026, victims' identities remained accessible despite assurances.
