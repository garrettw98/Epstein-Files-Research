# The Co-Conspirator Designation Process

> **Status**: **PARTIALLY DOCUMENTED / LEGAL INTERPRETATION SENSITIVE**
> **Purpose**: Clarify what "co-conspirator" labels in records do and do not establish

## Summary

In this repository, "co-conspirator" appears in multiple contexts: draft charging materials, internal FBI records, and later public unredactions. These references are important, but they are not all equivalent.

The key distinction:
- A **designation in records** is not the same as a **criminal charge**.
- A **criminal charge** is not the same as a **conviction**.

## Why This Topic Matters

Public discourse often collapses these categories, which creates two risks:

1. **Overstatement risk**: treating any designation as legal guilt.
2. **Understatement risk**: dismissing all designations as meaningless labels.

This topic provides a process map so claims stay accurate.

## Process Layers: What Each Layer Means

### Layer A: Investigative Mention

- Person appears in investigative records, correspondence, logs, or tips.
- Meaning: relevance for investigators.
- Does **not** imply criminal liability by itself.

### Layer B: Investigative Designation (e.g., "suspected co-conspirator")

- Internal categorization based on evidence evaluation at a point in time.
- Meaning: investigative posture.
- Does **not** equal a filed charge.

### Layer C: Charging Decision

- Prosecutors file criminal counts in court.
- Meaning: formal legal accusation.
- Still not proof of guilt until adjudication.

### Layer D: Adjudication

- Conviction by trial plea or verdict.
- Meaning: legal guilt established in court.

## Known Co-Conspirator Naming Disputes (2026 Cycle)

- Lawmakers argued that key non-victim names were over-redacted.
- DOJ stated later unredactions reflected non-victim name treatment.
- Named individuals or their counsel in some cases disputed the implication of being listed.

This means the designation process is both legal and reputational; context and supporting records are essential before inference.

## Research Standard for This Repo

When using co-conspirator terminology:

- Use one of these explicit labels:
  - **investigative designation**
  - **charged co-conspirator**
  - **convicted co-conspirator**
- Do not merge categories in summary language.
- Add record-level support (filing, memo, transcript, or official statement) before promoting claim severity.
- Include response statements from named individuals when available.
